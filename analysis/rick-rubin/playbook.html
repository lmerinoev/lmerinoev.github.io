Action Playbook

What to do Monday morning

01

Start a Daily Awareness Practice

Commit to 20 minutes of meditation every morning. Transcendental Meditation is Rubin’s practice of choice, but any form that quiets the mind and sharpens attention will work. Rubin has practiced daily since age 14 and credits it as the single most impactful thing he has ever done, more than any album, any collaboration, any career decision. The goal is not relaxation; it is clearing the lens of perception so you can see and hear what is actually there, not what you assume is there. If 20 minutes feels impossible, start with five. But start.

Source: Rubin’s meditation practice & “Cleaning the Lens” framework

02

Make Something for Yourself This Week

Create one thing with zero audience consideration. Write a song nobody will hear. Design a page nobody will see. Write a memo nobody will read. The point is to reconnect with authentic creative impulse, the thing you would make if no one were watching, if no metrics existed, if approval were irrelevant. Rubin’s central insight is that this is not self-indulgence; it is the only honest starting point. The paradox: making something purely for yourself is the most reliable path to making something that resonates with others.

Source: “The Audience Is You” framework

03

Remove One Element

Take your current project and strip away one element you think is necessary: a feature, a section, a layer, a decoration. See if the work is stronger without it. Rubin’s philosophy of reduction was most dramatically demonstrated with Johnny Cash: remove the band, the production, the studio gloss, and what remains is the truth. Most creative work suffers from addition, not subtraction. The question is never “what can I add?” but “what can I remove to make the essential visible?”

Source: Reduction / Stripping Away framework

04

Listen Without Analyzing

Put on a song, close your eyes, and just feel. Do not analyze the production, the mix, the chord progressions, the lyrics. Listen the way Rubin listens: as an audience member, not a technician. Does it move you? Does it feel true? Where does your attention drift? This is emotional listening, the skill that allows Rubin to cut through technical complexity and identify what actually matters. Practice it with music, then apply it to your own work and to the work of people you lead.

Source: “Producer as Audience” framework

05

Reset Your Environment

Change something physical about where you work. Blank the walls. Rearrange your desk. Remove decoration. Clear surfaces. Rubin’s Shangri-La studio is entirely white and stark, reset for each new session, because the environment shapes perception and perception shapes output. You do not need a Malibu studio. You need intention about your space. If your walls are covered with other people’s ideas, your mind will be too. Create blankness so that what fills the space comes from you.

Source: Shangri-La & environment-as-instrument philosophy

06

Write Down the Problems, Not the Solutions

For your current project, list everything that doesn’t feel right, without trying to fix anything yet. “The second verse loses energy.” “This section feels dishonest.” “I lose interest here.” Be specific about the feeling, vague about the fix. This is Rubin’s feedback method applied to your own work: diagnose before you prescribe. The act of naming what is wrong without rushing to solutions creates space for better solutions to emerge, often from unexpected directions.

Source: “Feedback as Emotional Diagnosis” method

07

Cross-Pollinate

This week, consume creative work from a domain completely unrelated to yours. Visit a museum if you work in music. Listen to an album if you work in design. Watch a documentary if you write code. Read a novel if you make spreadsheets. Rubin’s genre fluidity (producing hip-hop, metal, country, pop, folk, and comedy) is proof that creativity is universal and that the best ideas come from unexpected connections. Exposure to excellence in any domain can inspire creative work in your own.

Source: Genre fluidity & cross-disciplinary curiosity

08

Embrace Not Knowing

Identify one area where you are operating on assumption and treat it as if you know nothing. Approach it with beginner’s mind: no labels, no consequences, no past, no future. Rubin draws on the Zen concept of shoshin. Someone approaching a craft with a beginner’s mindset has a better chance of changing the game than an expert trapped in assumptions. The expert knows what’s impossible; the beginner doesn’t know enough to be constrained. Your expertise is an asset, but it is also a cage. Step outside it deliberately.

Source: Beginner’s Mind framework

“All that matters is that you are making something you love, to the best of your ability, here and now.”

— Rick Rubin
