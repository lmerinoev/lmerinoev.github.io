Personality Profile

Psychological traits, inner contradictions, and the personal philosophy of a barefoot guru who meditates daily and watches eight hours of professional wrestling weekly

Big Five Traits

  • Openness Very High
    The defining trait. Rubin’s entire career is an expression of radical openness: to unfamiliar genres (hip-hop producer working with country legends), to unconventional methods (lying on a couch with eyes closed during sessions), to non-Western philosophy (Buddhism, Taoism, Vedic traditions), to unexpected cultural passions (professional wrestling as “the most honest form of information in our society”). He follows his curiosity without regard for genre boundaries, conventional career logic, or others’ advice. “I’ve followed my intuition to make career turns, and been recommended against doing so every time.”
  • Conscientiousness Moderate
    A revealing split. Rubin is obsessive about the quality of perception — maintaining a daily meditation practice since age 14, carefully designing environments, attending to the subtlest emotional nuances in a performance. But he is famously laid-back in execution: lying on couches, conducting sessions barefoot, sometimes being physically absent from recordings he is credited with producing. The conscientiousness is directed inward (at the quality of attention) rather than outward (at the logistics of production). He cares deeply, but about different things than most producers care about.
  • Agreeableness High
    Rubin creates exceptionally safe psychological spaces for artists. His sessions are calm, non-confrontational, and judgment-free. He encourages experimentation without criticism. He does not impose solutions — he points out problem areas and trusts artists to resolve them. His collaboration philosophy is explicitly ego-less: “Most people in collaborations want their idea to win, but that’s not a good collaboration.” Artists consistently report feeling empowered rather than directed. Natalie Maines described him as having “the ability and the patience to let music be discovered, not manufactured.”
  • Neuroticism Low
    Rubin has practiced Transcendental Meditation since age 14 — nearly 50 years of daily practice. The result is a calm, grounded presence that is consistently described by collaborators as almost unnervingly serene. He speaks softly, moves slowly, and projects an unshakeable equanimity. He acknowledges the suffering that comes with artistic sensitivity but has built a comprehensive system for managing it: meditation, minimalism, nature, routine. His low neuroticism is not innate temperament but cultivated discipline.
  • Extraversion Low
    Rubin is reclusive by nature and preference. He speaks softly, prefers listening to talking, and gravitates toward one-on-one or small-group settings. Tim Ferriss called him “The Seclusive Zen Master.” His studios are isolated — The Mansion in Laurel Canyon, Shangri-La in Malibu — and his sessions are private, intimate affairs. He does not seek public attention, grants relatively few interviews, and when he does appear publicly, his manner is slow, deliberate, and quiet. His influence is exercised through presence and listening, not through charisma or persuasion.

The Meditator

Transcendental Meditation is not a hobby for Rubin — it is the foundation of everything else. He learned TM at age 14, when his doctor recommended it for stress-related neck pain. He was additionally motivated because the Beatles had learned TM from Maharishi Mahesh Yogi. He has stated that “starting meditation at 14 has had more impact on my life than anything else I have done.”

His daily practice: 20 minutes in the morning to center and ground himself; when possible, a second session before dinner. On 60 Minutes, the interview opened with Rubin leading Anderson Cooper through a brief meditation.

“It clears the distractions. The distractions can get in the way with a direct connection to the creative force.”

— Rick Rubin, 60 Minutes, 2023

The meditation practice is not separate from his production work — it is his production work, in a fundamental sense. TM allows him to be fully present with the artists he works with. It has trained him to be a deeply attentive listener — the core skill of his producer-as-audience methodology. Every creative framework he teaches — beginner’s mind, cleaning the lens, the Source — is essentially a restatement of what meditation teaches: clear the noise, and the signal reveals itself.

He is credited with getting Tim Ferriss into consistent meditation practice, suggesting the depth of his commitment to evangelizing the practice as foundational to creative and intellectual life.

The Contradiction

Rick Rubin is a walking collection of contradictions — and the contradictions are the point. They reflect a personality that refuses to be confined by categories, expectations, or conventional coherence.

  • A 320-pound vegan who gained weight through a “profound misinterpretation of the vegan approach” — consuming massive quantities of almond butter and other calorie-dense foods while believing he was eating healthily. Then lost 130 pounds in 15 months after switching to animal protein.
  • A metal producer who meditates. The man who produced Slayer’s Reign in Blood has practiced Transcendental Meditation daily since age 14. He moves between the most aggressive music on earth and the most serene inner practice without apparent contradiction.
  • A guru who watches eight hours of wrestling weekly. On Joe Rogan, Rubin described professional wrestling as “a really beautiful, fine art form” and “the most honest form of information in our society” — because it is openly constructed and makes no pretense of objectivity. For Rubin, transparency about artifice is more honest than media that pretends to objectivity while being equally constructed.
  • A music producer who knows nothing about music. He does not play instruments, cannot operate a mixing desk, and has no formal musical training. He has produced nine Grammy-winning albums.
  • A philosopher who resists explanation. He has written a 400-page book on creativity that critics call “supremely vague.” His entire method is to point out problems without providing solutions. He considers this a feature, not a bug.

These contradictions are not accidental — they are expressions of the same radical openness that defines his creative approach. A person who insists on coherence between their identities is a person constrained by categories. Rubin is not.

Eastern Philosophy, Western Expression

Rubin’s worldview is deeply rooted in Eastern philosophical traditions — Buddhism, Taoism, and Vedic thought — applied to predominantly Western creative expressions. This cross-pollination is the intellectual foundation of his work.

The Source/vessel framework echoes Vedantic concepts of Brahman flowing through individual consciousness. His emphasis on beginner’s mind comes directly from Zen Buddhism (shoshin). The natural-cycles framework reflects Taoist alignment with the universe’s unfolding. The Creative Act draws explicitly on all three traditions while addressing the practical concerns of any creator working in any Western medium.

The Way of Code (2025), his collaboration with Anthropic, made the Eastern roots explicit by reimagining the 81 chapters of the Tao Te Ching for the age of AI. He described it as “a 3,000-year-old wisdom teaching combined with cutting-edge technology.”

What makes Rubin unusual is not the Eastern influence itself — many Western creatives draw on Eastern thought. It is the completeness of the integration. His philosophy is not “Western production with some Buddhist seasoning.” It is a genuinely Eastern ontology applied to Western creative work: the artist does not originate, the artist receives; the goal is not perfection but authentic transmission; the primary instrument is not the tool but the self; the primary practice is not execution but perception.

The Health Transformation

For most of his career, Rubin’s physical presence was as distinctive as his philosophy: a large, bearded, barefoot figure in all black. At his heaviest, he weighed 320 pounds. He had been vegan from age 23 to 46, but the weight came from a misunderstanding of nutrition — consuming large quantities of calorie-dense foods under the assumption that “vegan” meant “healthy.”

The turning point was an intervention from legendary record executive Mo Ostin, who told Rubin he was going to die if he didn’t change his ways. The bluntness of the message broke through.

Rubin began eating animal protein — initially eggs and fish, “like medicine.” A nutritionist put him on a high-protein, low-calorie diet. He began working out six days a week with big-wave surfing legend Laird Hamilton. Dr. Phil Maffetone recommended 20 minutes of low heart-rate aerobic exercise daily. He used MyFitnessPal to track calories and become aware of hidden caloric intake.

The result: 130 pounds lost in 15 months.

The health transformation is consistent with the broader philosophy. Rubin’s central premise is that the quality of creative output is determined by the quality of perception, and that the self is the artist’s primary instrument. If the self is the instrument, then maintaining the instrument — physically, mentally, spiritually — is not separate from the creative work. It is the creative work. The same discipline of attention that he brings to listening in the studio, he eventually brought to his own body.
