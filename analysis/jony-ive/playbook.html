Action Playbook

What to do Monday morning

01

Audit Your Invisible Details

Pick one product or project. List every detail the user will never consciously notice — error states, packaging, loading animations, the underside, the sound something makes when it closes. Ask: does each one communicate care or carelessness? Fix the careless ones. Users can't articulate these details, but they feel them in aggregate.

Source: Care as First Principle / The Back of the Drawer

02

Write Before You Design

Before starting any design work, write 500 words about the problem you're solving. Not the solution — the problem. Who has this problem? What does their life look like with it? Without it? What assumptions are you making? The framing eliminates 60–70% of your solution space before you start. Make that elimination conscious.

Source: Writing as Problem-Framing

03

Build a Physical Prototype This Week

Even if your product is digital, make something physical. Print screens and hold them. Build a cardboard mockup. Shape foam into the form factor. The gap between what you imagine on a screen and what you hold in your hands is where the real design lives. Ive's insight is that your hands know things your eyes don't.

Source: Prototyping as Thinking

04

Create a Protected Space for New Ideas

Designate a time, place, or channel where half-formed ideas can be shared without being judged as finished proposals. This could be a weekly sketch review where nothing needs to be "ready," a shared doc where people post fragments, or a physical space with pinned work-in-progress. The goal is to lower the cost of sharing something vulnerable.

Source: The Fragility of Ideas

05

Replace "Is This New?" with "Is This Better?"

Review your current roadmap. For each planned change, ask: is this genuinely better for the user, or just different? Kill the "different" items. Novelty without improvement is waste. Improvement without novelty is craft. The latter is always more valuable.

Source: New vs. Different

06

Institute a Team Ritual

Start a recurring, low-stakes activity where your team makes something for each other — not work product, but something human. Cook a meal together. Share a skill. The point is not the activity but what it builds: the trust that allows people to share fragile ideas and challenge each other honestly.

Source: Friday Breakfast Ritual

07

Study Objects With Your Hands

Pick up three products you use daily. Close your eyes. What do you feel? The weight distribution, the surface texture, the way edges meet, the temperature of the material. Now do the same with a competitor's product. Train your sensitivity to the physical qualities that constitute "quality" — not through analysis but through attention.

Source: Taste as Trained Sensitivity

08

Count How Often You Say No

For one week, track every request, feature proposal, and meeting invitation you accept vs. decline. Ive's measure of focus is how often you say no. If you're saying yes to everything, you're not focused — you're reactive. True focus means ignoring things you care about in service of things you care about more.

Source: Focus as Saying No
