# Writing Standards

Style guide for articles on this site. Derived from the Merino/Elson/Derrick research papers (CHIRA 2021, HICSS 2021, HICSS 2022) and adapted for blog format.

## Voice

The academic papers use a formal but accessible register. For blog articles, keep the accessibility and drop the formality. The voice should be someone who knows the material well, explains it carefully, and doesn't overstate what they know.

**Carry over from the papers:**
- Measured claims. "Results suggest" not "results prove." Hedge where the evidence is incomplete.
- Define terms on first use. Don't assume the reader knows jargon. Give the definition in plain language.
- Explicit about uncertainty. "To the best of our knowledge" and "under the conditions of this study" are academic phrases, but the instinct behind them is right. Say what you don't know.
- Theory-grounded. When introducing a framework or concept, name it, state its core principle, then explain why it matters here.
- Practical implications. Every section should connect back to why someone would care.

**Adapt for blog:**
- Use "I" and "you" where natural.
- Shorter paragraphs. Academic papers run 8-12 sentence paragraphs. Blog should be 2-5 sentences.
- More variation in sentence length. The papers run 25-40 words per sentence consistently. Blog should mix short (5-10 words) with medium and long.
- Skip the exhaustive literature review. Reference specific researchers and findings but don't catalog every related paper.
- Conclusions can take a position. The papers are deliberately restrained. Blog articles can say what they think, as long as they're honest about the basis.

## Structure

### Introductions

Use the funnel pattern from the papers:
1. Broad context (one or two sentences about the state of the world)
2. Narrow to the specific problem or question
3. Why this matters (the gap, the stakes, the practical consequence)

Do NOT open with "In today's rapidly evolving world" or any variation. Start with something concrete.

### Sections

Each section should:
1. Name the concept or finding up front
2. Ground it (who found this, when, in what context)
3. Explain what it means in practice
4. Connect to the next section or the bigger picture

### Endings

Brief. Restate the main point if needed. Don't summarize everything you just said. The papers end with 2-4 sentences and a qualifier. Blog articles can be slightly longer but should resist the urge to recap.

## Language Rules

### Never use these words
delve, landscape (metaphor), tapestry, realm, paradigm, embark, beacon, testament to, robust, comprehensive, cutting-edge, leverage, pivotal, underscores, meticulous, seamless, game-changer, utilize, watershed moment, nestled, vibrant, thriving, deep dive, unpack, bustling, intricate, ever-evolving, enduring, daunting, holistic, actionable, impactful, learnings, thought leader, best practices, at its core, synergy, in order to, due to the fact that, serves as, features, boasts, commence, ascertain, endeavor, embrace (metaphor), symphony (metaphor)

### Never use these constructions
- Em dashes. Use commas, parentheses, or restructure the sentence.
- "It's not X, it's Y" / "It's not just X, it's also Y" inversions
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore" / "Let's break this down"
- "Here's the thing:" or "Here's why that matters:"
- Sentence-initial adverbs as insight signals ("Interestingly," "Notably," "Importantly," "Crucially")
- "It's worth noting that"
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" as paragraph openers
- "In conclusion" / "In summary" / "To sum up"
- Rule-of-three lists used as rhetorical flourish
- Colon-heavy openers ("The answer: [dramatic reveal]")

### Do this instead
- Use "is" and "has." Not "serves as," "stands as," "represents."
- Repeat the clearest word instead of cycling synonyms. If you're talking about trust, say trust. Don't alternate between trust, confidence, reliance, and faith to avoid repetition.
- Be specific. Names, dates, numbers, institutions. "Researchers at Wharton found" is better than "studies show."
- Vary sentence length deliberately. Mix short declarative sentences (5-10 words) with longer compound ones. Read it aloud; if every sentence has the same rhythm, rewrite.
- Vary paragraph length. Include some 1-2 sentence paragraphs alongside longer ones.

## Hedging

From the papers: hedging is not weakness. It's precision.

Use when the evidence is partial or contested:
- "Results suggest that..."
- "The evidence points toward..."
- "It is reasonable to expect..."
- "This finding, if replicated, could..."

Don't hedge when the evidence is strong or you're stating a definition:
- "Trust is a multidimensional construct." (Not "Trust is believed to be...")
- "The ReAct paper introduced the think-act-observe loop." (Not "The ReAct paper arguably introduced...")

## Citations and References

In blog format, weave references into the prose. Name the researchers and their institutions. Give the year. Don't use bracketed numbers or parenthetical author-year format.

Good: "Parasuraman and Manzey laid out the automation bias framework in 2010, and it's held up."
Good: "Kaur and colleagues studied this at CHI 2020 and found that even data scientists over-trusted the tools."
Bad: "Studies have shown that automation bias is a problem (Parasuraman & Manzey, 2010)."
Bad: "Research suggests that..." (no attribution)

## Formatting

- No callout/blockquote boxes with colored left borders
- No ASCII art diagrams. Use SVG if a diagram is needed.
- Code blocks are fine for actual code
- Use the takeaways section at the end sparingly. Keep items to one line each.
