# Writing Style Guide

Rules for all blog content on this site. Follow these strictly.

## Tone

Write like you're explaining something interesting to a smart friend. Be direct, clear, and honest. Respect the reader's time. You can be casual without being sloppy.

## Things to Avoid

### No em dashes

Don't use em dashes (-- or &mdash;) anywhere. Restructure the sentence instead. Use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses.

Bad: "The models appeared safe -- but they weren't."
Good: "The models appeared safe. They weren't."

### No "it's not X, it's Y" constructions

This pattern sounds like a TED talk. Just say what it is.

Bad: "The paper's contribution isn't proving deception exists, but rather showing our tools can't catch it."
Good: "The paper shows our current tools can't catch deceptive behavior if it arises."

Also avoid variations like:
- "It's less about X and more about Y"
- "The point isn't X. The point is Y."
- "This isn't just X. It's Y."

### No fluff adjectives

Cut words that don't add information. If you remove the adjective and the sentence still works, remove it.

Kill these on sight:
- groundbreaking, revolutionary, game-changing
- profound, fundamental, paradigm-shifting
- truly, deeply, incredibly, remarkably
- alarming, unsettling, chilling, stunning
- critical, crucial, essential, vital (when used for emphasis rather than accuracy)
- compelling, fascinating, intriguing
- novel, unique, unprecedented

### No hollow intensifiers

Words like "very", "extremely", "significantly", "particularly" usually signal that the sentence needs rewriting, not more adverbs.

Bad: "This is a particularly concerning result."
Good: "This result matters because [specific reason]."

### No rhetorical throat-clearing

Get to the point. Don't warm up.

Bad: "It's important to note that..."
Bad: "Perhaps the most counterintuitive finding is..."
Bad: "Here's the thing:"
Bad: "Several results stand out as particularly concerning:"
Good: Just state the finding.

### No "Imagine..." openings

Don't open sections with "Imagine..." or "Picture this..." or "What if...". These are filler. Start with the actual content.

### No "If there's a silver lining" or similar cliches

Avoid:
- "wake-up call"
- "the question now is whether..."
- "only time will tell"
- "at the end of the day"
- "the elephant in the room"
- "double-edged sword"
- "tip of the iceberg"
- "raises more questions than it answers"

### No "kitchen sink" metaphors or idioms when precision works better

Say what you mean directly.

Bad: "The researchers threw the kitchen sink of safety techniques at them."
Good: "The researchers applied every standard safety technique."

## Things to Do

### Lead with substance

Every paragraph should start with information, not setup. Front-load the interesting part.

### Use short sentences for impact

When something is important, say it plainly and briefly. Don't dress it up.

### Be specific

Replace vague claims with concrete details. Numbers, method names, and specific outcomes are better than adjectives.

### Let the research speak

When the findings are strong, you don't need to tell the reader how to feel about them. Present the result and trust the reader to understand its weight.

### Use "and" or periods instead of em dashes

If you're tempted to use an em dash, ask: is this a separate thought (use a period), a related clause (use "and" or a comma), or a clarification (use parentheses)?

### Keep paragraphs short

Three to five sentences max. One idea per paragraph. White space is your friend.

## Quick Self-Check

Before publishing, scan for:
1. Any em dashes? Rewrite.
2. Any "not X, but Y" patterns? Rewrite.
3. Any adjective you could delete without losing meaning? Delete it.
4. Any sentence that tells the reader how to feel? Rewrite to show, not tell.
5. Any paragraph longer than five sentences? Split it.
6. Any opening line that's throat-clearing? Cut it and start at the second sentence.
